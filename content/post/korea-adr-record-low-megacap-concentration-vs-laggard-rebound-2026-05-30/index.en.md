## TL;DR

* On May 29, 2026 the KOSPI set a fresh record at <strong>8,476pt (+3.55%)</strong>, but the same day's ADR fell to <strong>roughly 52%</strong> — the lowest since March 2020, a six-year trough. A record-high index paired with nine of ten stocks falling is an extraordinarily narrow market.
* The stars of this squeeze are Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix (the "megacap chip duo," or what Korean traders call "Jeon-Nik"). The starting point isn't a simple theme but a <strong>genuine memory earnings cycle</strong> — yet single-stock leverage ETFs piled a wave of speculative flow on top of it.
* The core conclusion is singular. <strong>Don't try to predict the unwind — confirm it with signals.</strong> The default right now is "concentration persists," and laggards should be added only when ADR, turnover, foreign flows, and earnings estimates all turn together.
* And the most important distinction: <strong>"riding the concentration" and "riding the concentration product (leverage ETFs)" are two completely different things.</strong>

## 1. What Is Actually Happening in the Korean Market

Start with the facts. ADR doesn't measure how high the index is — it measures <strong>the breadth inside the market</strong>.

```text
ADR = Advancing issues / Declining issues × 100
```

When ADR is 100, advancers and decliners are balanced. Well below 100 means "regardless of the index, most stocks are falling." The roughly 52% reading on May 29 is a level not seen since the 2020 pandemic crash.

| Item | As of May 29 |
|---|---|
| KOSPI | 8,476.15pt, +3.55%, all-time high |
| ADR | About 52%, a six-year low |
| Market breadth | A breadth collapse close to "nine of ten stocks falling" |

The previous note showed ADR at 67; in the interval, the market's internals narrowed further. In other words, the record-high index is <strong>not the result of a broadly strong market, but of a handful of megacaps doing the pulling</strong>.

### Two Flows That Amplified the Squeeze

Two distinct flows magnified the concentration.

<strong>First, single-stock leverage ETFs.</strong> On May 27 alone, total turnover in Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix single-stock leverage ETFs was about 10.4 trillion won, against a combined market cap of roughly 4.9 trillion won. Dividing turnover by market cap gives a daily turnover ratio of about 207%. That signals short-term, event-driven flow rather than normal long-term capital. The combined turnover of the four KODEX and TIGER Samsung Electronics/SK Hynix leverage products alone came to about 9.42 trillion won.

<strong>Second, the MSCI rebalancing.</strong> MSCI applied its May review at the May 29 close, and domestic brokers estimated 1.2 to 1.4 trillion won of passive inflows from higher weightings for SK Hynix and Samsung Electronics. Money that simply tracks the index was structurally forced to buy more of the duo.

In short, today's concentration is the product of <strong>real earnings + passive flows + leverage flows</strong> all aligning in one direction.

## 2. Two Interpretations

There are two broad ways to read this.

| Interpretation | One-line summary | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| A. The squeeze tightens further | Strong leaders run for a long time | Memory earnings cycle, passive and leverage flows, MSCI weight increases |
| B. It's the laggards' chance to rebound | A market this narrow snaps back | ADR at a six-year low; extreme breadth collapses don't last |

Both are partly right. A is correct in that "the strongest earnings momentum still sits with the duo," and B is correct in that "extreme ADR lows have statistically been followed by rebounds."

The real issue is <strong>what to do when both are true</strong>.

## 3. The Answer: Don't Predict — Confirm

Bottom line first: <strong>no one can know in advance</strong> when the concentration unwinds. Strong leaders always run longer than expected. So abandoning the leaders too early on the reasoning that "rotation into laggards is coming soon" usually costs you.

Instead, judge the unwind not by "has the duo rolled over," but by <strong>"has the rest of the market, ex-duo, gained the power to rise on its own?"</strong> Put plainly, these are the five things to watch:

1. <strong>Ex-duo relative strength</strong>: Does the KOSPI excluding the duo start to outperform the duo itself?
2. <strong>Broadening advancers</strong>: With the index holding, does the number of advancing stocks grow and does ADR recover above 70?
3. <strong>Broadening foreign flows</strong>: Do foreign net buys stop clustering in the duo and spread to other names?
4. <strong>Leverage ETF cooldown</strong>: Does leverage-ETF turnover cool while the underlying Samsung and SK Hynix shares hold firm?
5. <strong>Laggard estimate upgrades</strong>: Are the earnings estimates (EPS) of the neglected names being revised higher?

When three or four of these light up together, that's when you can acknowledge a rotation. There's no need to bet ahead of time with only one or two flashing.

### The Most Important Distinction: "Riding the Squeeze" vs. "Riding the Leverage ETF"

Here's a trap worth flagging. "I'll ride the concentration in the duo" and "I'll buy the duo's leverage ETF" are entirely different statements.

A single-stock leverage ETF is not a stock — it's a <strong>leverage product that resets daily</strong>. You're buying not SK Hynix itself but "twice SK Hynix's daily return," and the longer you hold, the more the two become completely different assets.

These products carry <strong>negative compounding (volatility decay)</strong>. Even before listing, Korea's Financial Supervisory Service warned that "if a price repeatedly rises and falls, cumulative returns can diverge sharply from the underlying." On the managers' own simulations, holding a high-volatility name at 2x leverage for about three months often produces a <strong>loss of typically 8% to 12% on the leverage ETF even when the underlying is flat (0%)</strong>.

The key is this: <strong>even if the squeeze doesn't unwind and the duo merely trades sideways, the leverage ETF loses over time.</strong> On top of that, with no diversification the daily limit is a wide ±60%, and on June 28 the FSS opened an investigation into possible wash trades and overheating. In other words, it's a product whose exit you can't easily control.

So if you want to ride the concentration, the right approach is <strong>the underlying (cash) shares, not chasing the leverage ETF</strong>. Riding negative compounding — a "publicly disclosed inefficiency" — isn't alpha; it's just leverage.

## 4. Investment Idea Examples (Observation Points, Not Recommendations)

The below are not stock recommendations but <strong>examples</strong> showing "what conditions make a name a candidate." This isn't a call to buy now, but a map of where to look first when the five confirmation signals above light up.

### Example 1 — The Next Bottleneck After GPU/HBM: Power, Substrates, Passives

As AI servers grow more complex, demand rises not only for GPUs and HBM but also for <strong>parts that stabilize power, substrates, and passive components</strong>. MLCCs, FC-BGA, and high-layer-count boards (MLB) are the prime examples. That said, some names here have already run a long way. One large component stock rose about 2.56x between late April and late May. So the point is <strong>not "what looks cheap" but "what is seeing fresh upward earnings revisions."</strong>

### Example 2 — Traces of Foreigners' Pre-Positioning

There's one intriguing signal. On May 29, as retail dumped en masse and memory melted up, <strong>foreigners quietly net-bought some deeply beaten-down materials, parts, and equipment names</strong>. Wonik IPS, Leeno Industrial, and EO Technics were among them. Because domestic institutions mostly sold alongside retail, a "foreigners buying, domestic institutions selling" divergence emerged. Whether that divergence resolves with domestic institutions returning is the second confirmation point.

### Example 3 — Where Does the Money the Leverage ETFs Bleed Go?

The most contrarian view is this: when everyone is buying the duo's leverage ETFs, watch <strong>where the money those products bleed via negative compounding will flow</strong>. When leverage holders bleed in sideways and choppy ranges, that money ultimately has to flow back into more efficient exposure — index products, cash equities, and neglected quality names. This is a bet on direction rather than timing, which makes it relatively safe.

> Common condition: all of the examples above need more than just "oversold." A name only becomes a candidate when <strong>earnings aren't impaired, estimates are being revised higher, and turnover and foreign flows are attaching for the first time</strong> — all at once.

## 5. The June Calendar Is the Real Variable for Rotation

The biggest enemy of a laggard rebound is, surprisingly, <strong>rates and liquidity</strong>. June is packed with heavyweight events.

| Event | Date | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| 9th nationwide local elections | June 3 | Short-term political uncertainty |
| US May CPI | June 10 | Whether inflation reignites |
| FOMC | June 16–17 | Chair Kevin Warsh's first meeting |
| SpaceX IPO (target) | June 12 | Assumed to be a major liquidity-absorbing event |

US April PCE inflation came in at +3.8% year over year, so the inflation burden is still not low. If June CPI accelerates further or the new Fed chair is read as hawkish, <strong>rates could rise and rotation into high-valuation laggards (especially high-PER component names) could be delayed</strong>.

And keep one statistical trap in mind. The claim that "the win rate of rebounds after extreme ADR lows is nearly 100%" omits that the sample is small and that most past cases occurred during <strong>rate-cutting regimes</strong>. We may instead be in the opposite regime, with inflation stirring again. You can't mechanically transplant past win rates onto the present.

## 6. Wrap-Up — Fund Manager's Comment

Back to the question: "Will the megacap chip squeeze tighten, or is it the laggards' chance to rebound?" The honest answer is <strong>"both are possible, and right now we're in the stage of waiting for the signal that separates them."</strong>

The two riskiest choices are:

* <strong>Abandoning the leaders too early on the back of a low ADR alone</strong> — handing over strong earnings momentum at a discount.
* <strong>Chasing an already-overheated single-stock leverage ETF too late</strong> — being dragged along with no exit by negative compounding and ±60% volatility.

So the most rational stance right now boils down to this:

> Hold the duo <strong>via the underlying (cash) shares</strong>, do not chase the leverage ETF, and add laggards <strong>only after confirming that ADR, turnover, foreign flows, and earnings estimates all turn together</strong>.

Unwinding a concentration isn't something you predict — it's something you confirm. Not missing the moment that first confirmation signal lights up, using the five-point checklist above — that's the most practical preparation you can make right now.
