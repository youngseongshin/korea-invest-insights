South Korea's benchmark KOSPI index fell approximately 4.4% to 6,518.79 in Monday's session — one of the steeper single-day declines of 2026 — as AI-related valuation pressure collided with rising geopolitical risk in energy markets. The selloff was broad but concentrated in semiconductor and high-growth names, with less than 15% of KOSPI components trading above their 50-day moving average and fewer than 20% above their 200-day line. This isn't a stock-by-stock correction. It's a regime repricing.

## Why Good AI News Couldn't Offset the Valuation Reset

The session's central tension: TSMC (2330.TW), the world's leading semiconductor foundry, reaffirmed strong AI demand signals and announced expanded investment in its Arizona operations. Under normal conditions, that would be a direct tailwind for Korean memory chipmakers who supply the high-bandwidth memory (HBM) powering AI accelerators. Today it wasn't enough.

The countervailing force was concern around Kimi K3, a next-generation Chinese AI model that sparked fresh debate about inference efficiency and its implications for hyperscaler capital expenditure. If AI compute becomes more efficient per dollar spent, the multi-year capex ramp that underpins the Korean memory bull thesis gets stress-tested. Markets responded not by abandoning the thesis, but by compressing the multiple they're willing to pay for it — a valuation de-rating that hit Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix even as TSMC's demand commentary remained constructive.

This is the recurring dynamic in AI infrastructure stocks: the underlying demand story stays intact while the growth premium oscillates on model competition news. International investors tracking Korean equities need to separate the fundamental thesis from the valuation cycle.

## Institutional vs. Foreign Flow Divergence: The Key Signal

The most informative data from Monday's session is the institutional-versus-foreign flow split in Korea's two flagship semiconductor names.

**Samsung Electronics (005930.KS)** — South Korea's largest company by market cap and the world's leading DRAM and NAND flash manufacturer — posted net foreign buying of approximately ₩276.2 billion against net institutional selling of ₩351.5 billion. The stock fell roughly 4.3% on the day, extending a five-session decline of approximately 30.6%.

**SK Hynix (000660.KS)** — the world's second-largest DRAM producer and the primary HBM supplier to Nvidia for its AI GPU platforms — saw an even starker divergence: foreign investors were net buyers of ₩612 billion while domestic institutions sold ₩934.3 billion net. The five-session decline stands at approximately 19.1%.

Why are foreign buyers stepping in while domestic institutions exit? One reading: longer-horizon EM-dedicated funds are treating the correction as an entry point on memory cycle fundamentals. Domestic institutions, facing nearer-term redemption pressure and risk management triggers, are reducing beta. Historically in Korean tech, sustained foreign buying into institutional distribution has sometimes marked the tail end of a correction — but that pattern requires volume expansion and breadth confirmation before it can be read as a reversal signal rather than a falling knife.

## One Outlier: FADU

**FADU (376300.KQ)**, a Korean SSD controller chip startup targeting enterprise storage and AI data center applications, closed up approximately 1.7% — the only name in the semiconductor analysis universe that gained on the day. The caveat: both foreign and domestic institutional flows were modestly net negative, suggesting the price move lacked conviction buying behind it. Without supportive flow, a single-day outperformance during a broad sector selloff is more noise than signal.

## Geopolitical Risk Layer: Hormuz and Crude

Compounding the valuation pressure is a macro risk that Korea is especially sensitive to: Strait of Hormuz tensions pushing Brent crude into the low $90s per barrel. Roughly 20% of global seaborne oil trade passes through the Strait. Korea imports nearly all of its energy needs, which means sustained elevated crude prices hit corporate margins across the manufacturing base, put pressure on the current account surplus, and historically weigh on foreign institutional appetite for KRW-denominated assets.

This isn't a short-term noise factor. If Brent holds above $90, the macro discount on Korean equities widens — independently of any semiconductor-specific catalyst.

## Where Relative Strength Is Hiding

The sectors posting relative outperformance Monday were energy, financials, and shipbuilding. This is a classic defensive rotation profile — not a signal of genuine conviction in those sectors, but rather investors reducing AI and growth beta and parking in names less sensitive to the Kimi K3 or capex-efficiency narratives.

Korean shipbuilding in particular has had structural tailwinds from LNG vessel orders and naval retrofitting demand. But the rotation into shipbuilding during a semiconductor selloff is better read as a risk-reduction trade than an upgrade to the order book outlook.

## Two High-RS Names on the Radar

Two mid-cap names emerged from Monday's relative strength screening:

**Tiger Electric (타이거일렉)** closed at ₩79,500 with a relative strength score of 97.7 — placing it in the top 3% of Korean stocks by price momentum — and volume running 2.49 times its 20-day average. A technically strong setup, but one that needs broader index participation to convert into a actionable breakout. The setup to watch: a clean move above the 20-day consolidation range accompanied by sustained institutional buying.

**TSE (티에스이)**, trading at ₩258,500 with a relative strength score of 97.8, shows similar characteristics. Given current breadth conditions — with only 14% of KOSPI above the 50-day moving average — high relative strength names tend to underperform expectations until the index itself stabilizes. These are setups to monitor, not chase.

## Key Levels for Tuesday's Session

Two technical levels define the near-term outlook for Korean semiconductor stocks:

- **Samsung Electronics**: ₩244,000 is the near-term support line. A confirmed close below this level would suggest institutional distribution is ongoing; recovery above would support the foreign buying thesis gaining traction.
- **SK Hynix**: ₩1,764,000 is the analogous marker. Given ₩934.3 billion of institutional selling in a single session, consecutive closes below this level would indicate the correction has more room to run.

## What to Watch

Korea's AI semiconductor trade is not broken — it is being repriced. The structural demand case for HBM (dominated by Samsung and SK Hynix, with little near-term competition) remains intact. The variable is the premium the market assigns to that demand, and that premium is under pressure from AI efficiency narratives and macro headwinds simultaneously.

For international investors, the setup is a textbook late-correction configuration: extreme breadth compression, divergent institutional and foreign flows, and a thesis that has moved from consensus bullish to actively contested. Tuesday's institutional flow data and price action around the two levels cited above will be the clearest read on whether Monday was the capitulation or merely the midpoint.
