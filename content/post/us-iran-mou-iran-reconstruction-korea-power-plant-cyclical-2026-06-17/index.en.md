Where the earlier notes looked at rates, foreign flows and re-rating separately, this one translates <strong>a new variable, the shrinking of Middle East risk,</strong> into Korea's cyclical value chain.

## TL;DR

* On the disclosed 14-article draft, this is <strong>a strategic win on points for Iran</strong>. Washington secured the reopening of Hormuz and an end to the war, but Iran's regime, its nuclear leverage and its path back to economic openness all survived.
* The real substance of "Iran reconstruction" is not reparations or aid but <strong>a private-investment (project-finance) pipeline that follows sanctions relief</strong>. The most immediate cash flow is not the $300 billion fund but <strong>the resumption of Iranian crude sales</strong>.
* From a Korean investor's standpoint this is a risk-on re-rating event that runs through <strong>power equipment, plant components, large EPC, shipbuilding, airlines and brokerages</strong>. But chasing small-cap reconstruction theme stocks is dangerous.
* The conclusion is single. This event should be read not as an "Iran reconstruction play" but as <strong>a smaller Middle East risk premium + a sanctions-relief option + a re-pricing of Korea's plant, power and shipbuilding value chain</strong>. The next moves to watch are OFAC waivers, the final MOU text, and confirmation of Korean firms' LOIs and order disclosures.

## 1. Scope and Core Questions

This analysis turns on five questions.

1. The disclosed 14-article U.S.-Iran MOU draft — <strong>whom does the document favor?</strong>
2. Are the military winner of the war and the winner of the negotiated text the same?
3. Is "$300 billion of Iran reconstruction" real money, or a political number?
4. Through what transmission channel does it work on the Korean stock market?
5. Should an investor today chase construction stocks, or position for a broader cyclical re-rating?

The premise must be stated clearly. What is public now is not a final treaty but <strong>a draft of MOU/framework character</strong>. Domestic reports citing Bloomberg say at least three drafts were discussed between Washington and Tehran, with the common denominators being the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz, eased Iran sanctions, and a long-term negotiation over Iran's nuclear program. ([Asia Business Daily][1])

## 2. The Core Structure of the 14-Article MOU

| Article cluster | Core content | Investor reading |
|---|---|---|
| Articles 1-3 | End of war on all fronts; sovereignty, territorial integrity, non-interference; negotiate a final accord within 60 days | Premised on the survival of Iran's regime. A signal that regime change is off the table |
| Articles 4-5 | U.S. lifts the naval blockade; Iran clears mines and normalizes merchant shipping | The global market secures Hormuz stability. Iran recovers its export route |
| Article 6 | A plan to raise at least $300 billion for reconstruction and economic development | The headline is large, but the substance is a private-investment / project-finance structure |
| Article 7 | Pledge to lift UN, IAEA and U.S. primary and secondary sanctions | The key article for reopening Iran's economy |
| Articles 8-9 | Ban on building nuclear weapons reaffirmed; enriched uranium and nuclear needs left to follow-on talks | Not an immediate dismantlement. Iran retains negotiating leverage |
| Articles 10-11 | Crude and petrochemical exports; exemptions for finance, insurance and transport; release of frozen assets | The most immediate economic benefit |
| Articles 12-14 | Set up an implementing body; final negotiation after conditions are met; approval by a UNSC resolution | If it becomes a final accord, legal binding force can strengthen |

The core is <strong>asymmetry</strong>. Iran offers "reaffirming the ban on building nuclear weapons, normalizing merchant shipping, joining the talks." The U.S. offers "lifting the naval blockade, allowing crude exports, a sanctions-relief path, releasing frozen assets, a reconstruction fund." This is not an instrument of surrender but <strong>a ceasefire text premised on the survival of Iran's regime and the reopening of its economy</strong>.

---

## 3. Who Won the War

### 3.1 Iran — A Strategic Win on Points

On the public draft, the biggest winner is Iran. What Iran obtained: regime survival, language on sovereignty and territorial integrity, the lifting of the naval blockade, exemptions for crude and petrochemical exports, the possibility of releasing frozen assets, a sanctions-relief roadmap, a $300 billion fund option, and follow-on talks rather than the immediate dismantlement of its nuclear program. Militarily it took damage, but politically it is <strong>the side that got hit and still survived</strong>. In an authoritarian system and in the security politics of the Middle East, that is a very large victory.

### 3.2 The United States — A Successful Loss-Limiter, Not a Complete Win

The U.S. obtained an end to the war, the reopening of Hormuz, oil-price stability, and a restart of nuclear talks. Axios reported that the U.S.-Iran framework includes a 60-day ceasefire extension, the reopening of Hormuz, and a restart of nuclear talks. ([Axios][2]) That said, what would have been America's top goals — regime change, immediate removal of enriched uranium, limits on ballistic missiles, dismantlement of the proxy structure — are not in the public text. Reuters likewise reported that the current MOU is not a final accord, and that Trump said in effect he could resume military action if he did not like the terms. ([Reuters][3]) So the U.S. is closer to <strong>a loss-limiter who built an exit off a position of military superiority</strong>.

### 3.3 Israel — A Relative Loser

The text is unfavorable to Israel. (1) The central parties to the ceasefire text are not Israel but the U.S. and Iran; (2) even the Lebanon front was folded into the ceasefire frame; and (3) ballistic missiles, Hezbollah and the proxy network were not nailed down as clauses for immediate, enforced dismantlement. The Guardian reported that in the G7 discussion it was raised that Iran's ballistic missiles and regional threats should be handled in follow-on negotiations. ([Guardian][4]) In short, <strong>the side that fought the war was Israel, but the pricing power over the ceasefire text rests with the U.S. and Iran</strong>.

### 3.4 Global Markets and the Gulf — Pragmatic Winners

From a market standpoint, the most important thing is the reduction of Hormuz risk. Reuters reported that after expectations of a deal, Gulf bourses mostly rose and Brent fell 5.1% to $82.86 a barrel. ([Reuters][5]) For Korea, an energy importer, this is broadly positive — a lower oil-price risk premium, eased inflation worries, a return of risk appetite, a restart of foreign risk-on, and cyclical rotation.

## 4. The Substance of Iran's Reconstruction

### 4.1 "Reconstruction" Is Not One Thing but Three

| Category | Substance | Certainty |
|---|---|---|
| Physical repair | War-damage repair of refineries, ports, airports, steel, the power grid, etc. | Medium |
| Economic reopening | After eased sanctions, restart of investment in energy, logistics, manufacturing, transport | High, but conditional |
| Nuclear-deal incentive | The reward package when Iran meets its nuclear, inspection and Hormuz conditions | High, but dependent on a final accord |

The important point is that the $300 billion is <strong>not a U.S. cash reparation or ODA</strong>. Reuters reported that the fund is wholly private-money-based and targets energy, logistics, manufacturing and transport, and that it is not U.S. taxpayer money or a government subsidy. ([Reuters][6])

### 4.2 The Nature of the $300 Billion Fund

<strong>Verdict: there is substance, but right now it is closer to a conditional investment option than a hard commitment.</strong> Per Reuters reporting, the fund is wholly private money, open to firms from the U.S., the Gulf, Asia, South America and Africa; an anonymous source says more than half is committed; the targets are energy, logistics, manufacturing and transport; disbursement follows a final accord; and the structure depends on Iran's nuclear, inspection and regional-security compliance. In VC terms, what exists now is <strong>not a signed capital call but soft-circled capital + a political option</strong>.

### 4.3 The Money With the Most Substance Is Crude

Right after the MOU is signed, the thing that can be turned into cash fastest is not the reconstruction fund but <strong>the resumption of Iranian crude sales</strong>. Reuters reported that the U.S. agreed, upon conclusion of the MOU, to allow waivers for Iran's crude and fuel sales and the related banking, shipping and insurance services, and that Iran could access more than 100 million barrels of stored crude. ([Reuters][7])

```text
Short-term crude monetizable gross value
= 100 million barrels of stored crude x Brent ~$79/barrel
= ~$7.9 billion

Check: 100,000,000 barrels x $80/bbl = $8.0bn ~ $7.9bn
```

This sum is smaller than $300 billion, but its <strong>immediacy, liquidity and feasibility of execution are far higher.</strong>

### 4.4 Crunching the Numbers

| Item | Formula | Result | Reading |
|---|---|---:|---|
| Reconstruction fund / GDP | 300bn / 300.29bn | 99.9% | Close to a full year of Iran's 2026 nominal GDP |
| Half-committed assumption | 150bn / 300.29bn | 50.0% | Anonymous-source basis; legal binding force unconfirmed |
| Reconstruction fund / energy-repair cost | 300bn / 58bn | 5.17x | Not mere repair cost but an industrial-reopening package |
| Stored-crude gross value | 100m barrels x ~$79 | ~$7.9bn | The most immediate cash flow |

The IMF puts Iran's 2026 nominal GDP at $300.29 billion, real growth at -6.1%, and inflation at 68.9%. ([IMF][8]) Rystad Energy estimated that repairing energy infrastructure from the Middle East war could cost up to $58 billion, of which oil & gas facilities could be up to $50 billion. ([Reuters][9])

## 5. The Transmission Path Into the Korean Stock Market

### 5.1 First Channel — Lower Oil Prices

Korea is a net energy importer. A smaller Hormuz risk and a resumption of Iranian crude sales are broadly positive for the macro.

| Sector | Impact |
|---|---|
| Airlines | Lower fuel cost -> margin improvement |
| Chemicals | Lower naphtha price -> possible relief in input costs |
| Refining | Falling crude is an inventory-valuation-loss risk. Need to check refining margins |
| Brokerages | Risk-on, higher trading value |
| Semiconductors | Not a direct beneficiary, but a supporting variable via foreign risk-on |

But refining is not a simple beneficiary. Falling crude can create inventory valuation losses, and the real profit is decided by the refining margin (crack spread).

### 5.2 Second Channel — Risk-On in the Korean Market

The Korean market has already reacted once. Yonhap reported that on June 15, 2026 the KOSPI rose 5.2% to close at 8,545.98 on the U.S.-Iran agreement to end the war and expectations of Hormuz's reopening, and that the next day it added 2.11% to close at 8,726.6. ([Yonhap News][10]) This reaction is not because of Iran reconstruction alone — a smaller Middle East tail risk, a lower oil-price premium, a possible easing of the burden on the U.S. rate path, a recovery of foreign risk-on, higher trading value, and cyclical rotation all worked together. So looking only at "Iran reconstruction plays" is too narrow. The bigger frame is <strong>a Korean cyclical re-rating</strong>.

### 5.3 Third Channel — The Reconstruction / Plant / Power-Equipment Option

The reconstruction fund's targets, as reported by Reuters, are energy, logistics, manufacturing and transport. ([Reuters][6]) These four areas overlap with fields where Korean firms are strong (refining and petchem plants, gas processing, power generation and transmission/distribution, ports, airports and logistics, steel and manufacturing equipment, shipbuilding and offshore plant, plant components). The core is <strong>not general construction but EPC, power and components</strong>.

### 5.4 Fourth Channel — The Gateway of Sanctions, Settlement and Insurance

For actual investment to be possible, U.S. OFAC documents are needed. As of June 2026, OFAC still keeps the Iran sanctions as an active program. ([OFAC][11]) For Korean firms to move, at minimum the following must be clear: (1) crude and petchem trade waivers, (2) permission for bank settlement, (3) permission for shipping, insurance and reinsurance, (4) permission for EPC contracts, advance payments and guarantees, (5) the scope of the SDN trade ban, (6) whether dollar settlement is possible, and (7) whether secondary-sanctions immunity applies. Without these documents, an order may be "news" but it is not earnings.

## 7. Market Reaction and Current Position

The Korean market has already reacted once. The Seoul Economic Daily reported that on the MOU news construction, airline and brokerage stocks rose together, with Samsung E&A up 10.71%. ([Seoul Economic Daily][12]) So the judgment now is this.

| Bucket | Investment call |
|---|---|
| Construction stocks that already surged | Need pullback / confirmation rather than chasing |
| Airlines, brokerages | Short-term-trade in character. Check oil and trading value |
| Power equipment, plant components | Core medium-term basket |
| Large EPC | Add weight after OFAC waivers and actual LOIs |
| Small-cap reconstruction theme | Avoid first |

The Korean government has also been reported as not yet having received a formal request from the U.S. and as taking a stance of monitoring the situation. ([Seoul Economic Daily][13]) The Hankyoreh likewise reported that while Korean firms' participation was reported, specific company names, amounts and binding force have not been disclosed. ([Hani English][14]) In other words, it is still too early to price in "confirmed Korean-firm participation."

## 8. Investment Strategy

### 8.1 Short Term — Verify Rather Than Chase

The construction, airline and brokerage stocks that have already had a first surge are an event-driven reaction. What matters is not the feeling of having missed out but <strong>the order in which substance gets confirmed</strong>. The principles: (1) no chasing the surged construction stocks; (2) for oil-beneficiary names, check cost and margin sensitivity; (3) for brokerages, confirm the persistence of trading value; (4) for defense stocks, factor in a possible partial reduction of the Middle East premium; and (5) semiconductors can benefit from risk-on flows separate from Iran reconstruction.

### 8.2 Medium Term — Centered on Power Equipment and Plant Components

Over the medium term, the most reasonable are: (1) power equipment, cable, transmission/distribution; (2) plant components; (3) among large EPC, firms with good financials, order backlog and cost ratios; and (4) among shipbuilders/offshore, firms with tanker, LNG and offshore-plant options. The reason is simple. Even if Iran reconstruction is delayed, this basket has separate structural demand from <strong>AI data centers, the U.S. power grid, Middle East infrastructure and global energy capex</strong>.

### 8.3 After a Final Accord — Numbers, Not Themes

Once a final accord is signed and Korean company names are disclosed, from then on it must be seen in numbers.

```text
Contract value = contract amount x expected operating margin x collectability x sanctions-risk discount rate
```

Even a $1 billion order, with a 3% operating margin, 30% collection risk and large sanctions risk, leaves limited shareholder value. Conversely, even a $300 million component order, at a 15% operating margin with clear advance payments, LCs and insurance, is worth more.

## 10. Risk Factors

* <strong>Political risk</strong>: The MOU is not a final accord. Trump was reported to have said in effect that he could take military action again if he did not like the deal. ([Reuters][3])
* <strong>Nuclear-talks risk</strong>: The ban on building nuclear weapons gets documented, but enriched uranium, inspection and nuclear needs are for follow-on talks. If it breaks down there, the reconstruction fund and sanctions relief could be delayed or fall through.
* <strong>Sanctions risk</strong>: If OFAC documents are unclear, Korean firms find it hard to move. If bank, insurance, reinsurance and dollar settlement are uncertain, EPC contracts stay headlines rather than earnings.
* <strong>Investor-overheating risk</strong>: A "leading Iran reconstruction stock"-style small-cap rally can see flows move ahead of substance. The upside is large but the loss is fast. From an institutional standpoint it is better to avoid.

    Iran reconstruction did not hand the Korean market a construction-stock theme; through the removal of the Middle East risk premium and a sanctions-relief option, it handed it a rationale for a cyclical re-rating that runs through power equipment, plants, shipbuilding, airlines and brokerages.

The most reasonable actions now are these.

1. <strong>No chasing of small-cap reconstruction theme stocks</strong>
2. <strong>Keep power equipment, cable and plant components as the core basket</strong>
3. <strong>Select large EPC only after OFAC waivers and actual LOIs</strong>
4. <strong>Approach airlines and brokerages only as short-term trades</strong>
5. <strong>For refining and chemicals, check the crack spread and product spreads rather than oil prices</strong>
6. <strong>Read semiconductors not as an alternative Iran-reconstruction theme but as a supporting risk-on beneficiary</strong>

The winner on the text of the war is Iran, the short-term market winner is risk-on assets, and the Korean investor's medium-term alpha lies in power equipment, plant components and high-quality EPC.
