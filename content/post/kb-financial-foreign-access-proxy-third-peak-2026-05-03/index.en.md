<strong>Flow sensitivity math:</strong>


## 6. Two Honest Limits

Constructive tone shouldn't mean overstating the model. Two real limits:

### 6.1 Foreign 75% Is Two-Sided Infrastructure

Foreign ownership at 75.72% is KB's largest asset and largest exposure simultaneously.

<strong>On the asset side</strong>: when global passive and active capital flows into Korea, the auto-buy channel works. Governance friction is low, ESG fund entry is easy, baseline flow is stable.

<strong>On the exposure side</strong>: in EWY redemption cycles, EM risk-off, or won weakness regimes, KB is the Korean financial sold first. The higher the foreign ownership, the larger the "exit capacity" relative to the "entry capacity." In a market correction, KB likely experiences the largest foreign selling among the four bank holdcos.

This isn't a model defect — it is <strong>a structural feature of the "foreign access proxy" model itself</strong>. Same kind of model-identity feature as Meritz's insurance/securities capital sensitivity or Kiwoom's trading-volume volatility.

### 6.2 The ROE 10%-Class Ceiling

KB cannot become Meritz at ROE 22% or Kiwoom at ROE 20%. Bank holdcos are structurally pinned in the 9–11% ROE range. CET1 capital ratios, RWA growth dynamics, and credit-cycle mean reversion all set the ceiling.

This is less a limit and more <strong>the model's identity</strong>. What KB sells isn't "Meritz-class ROE" — it is <strong>Korea exposure, the capital-return package, and passive stability</strong>. Lower ROE ceiling, lower price (PBR), so end-state capital efficiency converges with the higher-ROE peers via a different route.

Accepting that, KB's identity becomes clearer. KB is not the same kind of company as Meritz or Kiwoom. It is a peak on a different dimension.
