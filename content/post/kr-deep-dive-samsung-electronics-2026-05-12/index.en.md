# Samsung Electronics: HBM, Galaxy, and the Foundry Comeback Story

**Samsung Electronics Co., Ltd. (005930.KS / 005935.KS preferred)** is the single largest constituent of the KOSPI index and one of the few companies on earth that simultaneously competes at the frontier of memory chips, logic foundry, consumer electronics, and mobile networks. For international investors building exposure to the AI infrastructure cycle, Samsung is impossible to ignore — and equally impossible to evaluate without confronting a genuine strategic tension at its core. This post unpacks both sides.

**Elevator pitch:** Samsung Electronics is the world's largest memory chip maker by revenue, the second-largest contract chipmaker by capacity, and the maker of the best-selling Android smartphone line on the planet. It sits at the intersection of three megatrends — AI-driven HBM demand, premium mobile, and the West's push to diversify semiconductor supply chains away from Taiwan. No single company offers a comparable breadth of exposure to the global technology hardware stack at a sub-10× forward earnings multiple.

## 2. The Global Story

### Why Non-Korean Investors Should Pay Attention

The 2024–2026 AI infrastructure build-out has reshuffled the semiconductor pecking order more violently than any cycle since the smartphone era. Nvidia's GPU roadmap, hyperscaler capex, and the race to deploy large language models at scale all converge on one physical bottleneck: **High Bandwidth Memory (HBM)**. Every H100, H200, and B200 GPU ships with HBM stacks. Every future Blackwell Ultra and Rubin will need more of them.

Samsung is one of only three companies in the world — alongside SK Hynix and Micron — that can manufacture HBM at commercial scale. It is the only one that also controls a leading-edge logic foundry (Samsung Foundry, 4nm GAA), a display panel business supplying Apple, and a device business that ships \~250 million smartphones per year. That breadth is both the bull and the bear case.

### The Global Trend

The primary demand driver for the next 12–24 months is **HBM3E and HBM4 qualification with Nvidia, AMD, and hyperscaler custom silicon teams**. Secondary drivers include:

- **AI-on-device (Galaxy AI):** Samsung is integrating on-device AI into the Galaxy S26 line, which lengthens the premium smartphone upgrade cycle and justifies higher ASPs.
- **Geopolitical diversification:** The CHIPS Act and its Korean equivalent push hyperscalers to qualify non-TSMC foundry sources. Samsung Foundry is the most credible alternative at 3nm–4nm.
- **Memory upcycle validation:** Q1 2026 earnings delivered a material upside surprise — internal briefing data from April 7 noted the print was "received not just as strong results but as numerical confirmation of the memory upcycle."

### Competitive Position

| Segment | Samsung Rank | Key Rival(s) |
|---|---|---|
| DRAM (revenue) | #1 globally | SK Hynix, Micron |
| NAND Flash (revenue) | #1 globally | Kioxia/WD, Micron |
| HBM (volume/revenue) | #2, closing gap | SK Hynix (#1) |
| Logic Foundry (capacity) | #2 globally | TSMC (#1), Intel Foundry |
| Smartphone (units) | #1 globally | Apple (#2 by revenue) |
| Display (OLED revenue) | #1 globally | LG Display, BOE |

The honest competitive caveat: in HBM specifically, SK Hynix holds a meaningful first-mover advantage — its HBM3E was qualified by Nvidia earlier and at higher yields. Samsung's internal research journal (as of April 2026) explicitly notes that "in terms of memory alpha efficiency, the case for partial reallocation to SK Hynix is becoming clearer." This is not a fatal flaw but it is a real nuance.

## 3. Business Model & Revenue Drivers

Samsung reports in four major segments. Based on recent filings available via **DART (dart.fss.or.kr)** and KRX disclosures:

### Segment Overview (approximate FY2025 mix)

| Segment | Revenue share | Key products |
|---|---|---|
| **DS (Device Solutions)** | \~45–50% | DRAM, NAND, HBM, Foundry (Logic) |
| **MX (Mobile Experience)** | \~30–35% | Galaxy S/A/Z series, tablets, PCs |
| **VD/DA (Consumer Electronics)** | \~10–12% | QLED/OLED TVs, home appliances |
| **Harman** | \~5–7% | Connected car audio, automotive electronics |

The DS division is the profit engine and the volatility driver. In a memory down-cycle (2022–2023), Samsung posted operating losses in DS. In the current upcycle, DS margins have recovered sharply, with Q1 2026 delivering a positive earnings surprise that moved the KOSPI.

### Key Growth Drivers (12–24 Months)

1. **HBM4 ramp:** Samsung is targeting HBM4 qualification with Nvidia for H2 2026. If successful, this closes the gap with SK Hynix and opens a multi-billion-dollar annual revenue stream that carries 40%+ operating margins at full utilization.

2. **Foundry 2nm GAA:** Samsung's SF2 (2nm gate-all-around) node is targeting risk production in 2026. Customer wins at this node — particularly from Qualcomm, AMD, and potentially Apple — would structurally re-rate the foundry segment.

3. **Galaxy AI premium cycle:** The Galaxy S26 and Z Fold series embed on-device generative AI at a price point that pushes ASP higher. Management has guided for premium mix expansion in MX through 2026.

4. **Long-Term Agreements (LTAs):** According to internal research (April 2026), Samsung has been building LTA structures with hyperscaler customers for DRAM supply. These reduce cyclical revenue volatility and improve capital allocation visibility.

### Margin Profile

- **DS gross margin:** Recovering toward the 40–45% range as HBM and server DRAM mix rises; NAND profitability lags DRAM.
- **MX gross margin:** Structurally lower (high-teens to low-20s%) but stable; premium mix improvement is the lever.
- **Foundry:** Still dilutive at sub-scale utilization rates; a sustained 60%+ utilization rate is required for breakeven in meaningful terms. This remains the biggest near-term drag.
- **Consolidated operating margin:** Recovering toward the 15–18% range in FY2026 from near-zero in FY2023.

## 4. Bull Case

### Catalyst 1: HBM4 Nvidia Qualification (H2 2026)

Samsung has publicly targeted HBM4 qualification with Nvidia for the Rubin GPU platform. If Samsung achieves this on schedule, it becomes a co-equal supplier alongside SK Hynix at the most critical node in the AI compute stack. The TAM for HBM is forecast by multiple industry trackers to exceed US$30 billion annually by 2027. A 30–35% Samsung share would represent a step-change in DS division revenue quality.

**Quantifiable signal to watch:** Monthly memory export data from Korea's Ministry of Trade (released first business day of each month). A sustained acceleration in high-value memory exports is the fastest leading indicator.

### Catalyst 2: Foreign Investor Re-entry

Internal flow data through May 12, 2026 shows Samsung's price momentum and institutional flows are "diverging" — price has held but foreign buying has been inconsistent. Samsung carries the highest weight of any single name in the MSCI Korea index (\~22–24%). When global EM funds rotate back into Korea (typically on a weaker USD or improving Korea-specific macro), Samsung absorbs an outsized mechanical bid. At ₩279,000, the stock is still \~30% below its 2021 all-time high, meaning a re-rating to prior peak would require no fundamental upside at all.

### Catalyst 3: Foundry Recovery + Strategic Restructuring

Samsung has been conducting an internal review of its foundry strategy following customer losses in 2024–2025. Any credible announcement of a strategic partnership (e.g., with a major US hyperscaler for dedicated capacity), a joint venture structure, or a clearer separation of foundry P&L would be a significant positive re-rating event. Precedent: Intel's foundry spin-off narrative drove multiple expansion before execution risk reasserted itself.

## 5. Bear Case

### Risk 1: HBM Yield and Qualification Delay

Samsung missed HBM3E qualification windows with Nvidia in 2024, ceding meaningful share and first-mover economics to SK Hynix. The same risk exists for HBM4. If Samsung's packaging yields (particularly 12-high stacks with Through Silicon Via integration) remain below Nvidia's threshold through H1 2026, the qualification timeline pushes to 2027 — and the market will price that in sharply. This is the highest-conviction near-term risk flagged in internal research as of April 2026.

### Risk 2: Foreign Exchange and Macro Regime

The USD/KRW rate was trading near ₩1,500 as of the internal briefing (April–May 2026), a level that signals Korea macro stress. Samsung derives the majority of its DS revenue in USD/contracts but reports in KRW. A sustained strong dollar is actually a partial revenue tailwind, but the broader macro regime — elevated global rates, US Bear market signals (VIX \~25), Brent crude at \~US$110 — creates risk-off pressure that disproportionately affects high-beta Korean tech names. Capital allocation discipline matters more in this environment.

### Risk 3: Foundry Customer Concentration and TSMC Competitive Moat

Samsung Foundry's largest logic customers (Qualcomm for some designs, Nvidia for a subset of older nodes, various fabless players) have consistently migrated leading-edge work back to TSMC when yield and schedule reliability diverge. TSMC's N3/N2 yield advantage over Samsung's SF3/SF2 is estimated by industry observers at 10–20 percentage points at equivalent ramp maturity — a gap that translates directly into customer economics. Until Samsung demonstrably closes this gap, foundry will remain a value-destructive segment that the market discounts heavily relative to TSMC's premium multiple.

| P/B | \~1.0–1.2× | \~1.8–2.2× | \~6–8× | \~2.0–2.5× |
| EV/EBITDA | \~5–7× | \~7–9× | \~14–17× | \~6–8× |

Samsung trades at a **persistent conglomerate discount** — the foundry drag, the complexity of managing four distinct businesses simultaneously, and governance concerns (cross-shareholding structure, family control) all compress the multiple versus pure-play peers.

The bull case argues this discount is excessive: you are buying the world's largest memory franchise plus an option on foundry recovery at a price that assumes foundry is worth approximately zero. The bear case argues the discount is structural and will not compress until Samsung demonstrates HBM4 execution and foundry profitability simultaneously.

**Versus history:** Samsung has traded in a P/B range of 1.0–2.5× over the past decade. At the current \~1.1× P/B, the stock is near the lower end of its historical range — typically a level associated with memory down-cycles or crisis periods. The current level is anomalous given that memory is in an upcycle, suggesting the market is applying a forward discount for foundry risk and HBM execution uncertainty.

## 7. How to Access This Stock

### ADR / GDR

Samsung Electronics does not have a US-listed ADR. However, it trades on the **OTC market** as **SSNLF** (common shares) and **SSNNF** (preferred shares) in the US, though OTC liquidity is limited and spreads can be wide. For meaningful position sizing, direct investment through the Korean Stock Exchange (KRX) via a broker with Korea market access is the standard approach.

### Key ETFs with Samsung Exposure

| ETF | Samsung Weight (approx.) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **iShares MSCI South Korea ETF (EWY)** | \~20–24% | Largest and most liquid Korea ETF |
| **Franklin FTSE South Korea ETF (FLKR)** | \~18–22% | Lower cost alternative |
| **iShares Semiconductor ETF (SOXX)** | Small allocation | Included in global semiconductor basket |
| **VanEck Semiconductor ETF (SMH)** | Small allocation | Smaller weight vs. US peers |

EWY is the most practical instrument for investors who want Korea exposure without direct market access, but Samsung's \~22% weight means the ETF is highly correlated to Samsung's performance.

### Practical Notes for Foreign Investors

1. **Settlement:** KRX operates on a T+2 settlement cycle. Foreign investors must open a Foreign Investment Registration (FIR) through the Financial Supervisory Service (FSS) — a one-time process typically handled by your Korean custodian or prime broker.

2. **FX:** All settlement is in Korean Won (KRW). USD/KRW volatility (\~1,400–1,500 range in 2026) adds a currency layer to equity returns. USD-based investors have experienced meaningful FX drag in recent quarters.

3. **Disclosure language:** All official filings are submitted to **DART (dart.fss.or.kr)**. Samsung publishes English-language earnings releases and IR materials on its investor relations site (ir.samsung.com), making it one of the most accessible Korean large-caps for non-Korean speakers.

4. **Preferred shares (005935):** Samsung's preferred shares trade at a \~10–15% discount to common shares, carry no voting rights, but receive a slightly higher dividend. For income-oriented investors, the preferred may offer a more attractive entry point.

5. **Dividend:** Samsung has historically returned capital via dividends and buybacks. The dividend yield on the common is modest (1–2% range) but the company has run significant buyback programs in periods of balance sheet strength.
