# Pearl Abyss (263750.KQ): 5 Million Copies Sold — Why the Real Question Is No Longer "How Many?" but "How Long?"

Crimson Desert crossed the <strong>5 million copies</strong> milestone on April 15, confirmed via Pearl Abyss's official social channels. That's 100 million additional copies in just 14 days since the company formally announced 4 million on April 1. The game continues to sell on PS5, Xbox Series X|S, Steam, Mac, and Epic Games Store — a truly multi-platform footprint built on Pearl Abyss's proprietary BlackSpace Engine.

But if you're still framing the Pearl Abyss thesis around "will it hit 7.5 million?", you're asking the wrong question. The math has moved on.

---

## TL;DR

- <strong>7.5 million copies is no longer the upside — it's closer to the floor.</strong> With 5M confirmed as of April 15 and a recent run-rate of \~71K copies/day over the last official tracking window, reaching 7.5M requires only \~9,600/day for the remainder of the year. The gap between current velocity and required pace is enormous.
- <strong>The real unlock is 9–10 million copies + a formal DLC/multiplayer roadmap.</strong> Free updates matter not for their direct revenue but for their ability to <strong>flatten the decay curve</strong> and extend the revenue tail into 2027.
- <strong>BlackSpace Engine is proven. Dokabi is still an option.</strong> The engine has graduated from tech demo to commercial AAA infrastructure — but the bulk of any market-cap re-rating still comes from Crimson Desert's post-2027 cash flows. Dokabi is option value at this stage, nothing more.

## 1. Fact Check & Structural Breakdown

<strong>Confidence level: High.</strong>

Sales milestones, the update roadmap, engine/R&D disclosures, and management commentary are well-documented. What remains unconfirmed: <strong>DLC pricing, scale, and attach rates; Dokabi's launch window; and Crimson Desert's 2027 decay rate.</strong>

Here's the factual foundation:

Crimson Desert launched globally on March 20, 2026 across PS5, Xbox Series X|S, Steam, Mac, and Epic Games Store. Pearl Abyss led with its BlackSpace Engine's high-fidelity graphics, dynamic combat, and environmental interactivity. The company officially announced 4 million copies sold on April 1, then confirmed 5 million via official social media on April 15. Between April and June, the company has committed to sequential free updates including boss re-challenges, reconquest systems, difficulty settings, new skills, new costumes, dedicated storage, new pets/mounts, and control/UI/draw-distance improvements — with the caveat that details may change as features are still in development.

Translated into capital flow, the structure is straightforward:

<strong>Base game sales → installed base expansion → free updates to sustain retention/reviews → DLC/multiplayer attach → lower discount rate on follow-up IP.</strong>

The two choke points: First, can BlackSpace Engine actually support large-scale open-world AAA in live production? Second, can the live-ops team absorb weekly user feedback fast enough to bend the decay curve? So far, both have passed their initial validation.

Breaking it down into <strong>P × Q × C</strong>: <strong>P</strong> = base game ASP by region plus future DLC pricing. <strong>Q</strong> = cumulative sales, monthly exit-rate, and DLC attach rate on the installed base. <strong>C</strong> = platform commissions, marketing, and post-launch live-ops costs. The current upside is driven more by <strong>Q than P</strong> — not "how much per copy" but "how long before the curve breaks." This is the fundamental difference from a mobile/MMO valuation framework.

## 2. Multi-Dimensional Analysis

### Thesis 1: 7.5 Million Is Now the Floor, Not the Ceiling

<strong>Classification: Idiosyncratic Alpha</strong>

As of April 15, we're at 5 million. To reach 7.5M by year-end requires selling 2.5M more over \~260 days — roughly <strong>9,600 copies per day</strong>. Meanwhile, the most recent officially trackable window (April 1 at 4M → April 15 at 5M) implies about <strong>71,000 copies per day</strong>. That pace won't hold indefinitely, but the gap between required and observed velocity is striking.

In our assessment, <strong>7.5 million is no longer "how high can it go?" — it's "what's the minimum it can hold?"</strong>

Translating to share price: our internal FY26 earnings sensitivity model puts <strong>7.8M copies as a base case at approximately KRW 74,500</strong>, with <strong>sensitivity of roughly KRW 10,000 per 1 million copies</strong>. So 7.5M alone doesn't trigger a dramatic multiple re-rating — it justifies the <strong>low-to-mid KRW 70,000 range</strong>. The real upside begins at <strong>9 million</strong>: that's +1.2M above base, implying the <strong>high KRW 80,000s</strong> on sensitivity alone. At 10 million, we're looking at <strong>KRW 100,000 territory</strong>.

The market's persistent mispricing: the <strong>"launch spike → immediate decay" framework</strong> that Korean investors reflexively apply to packaged games. The math currently says the opposite. 7.5M is too low a bar. <strong>The debate should center on 900K–1M and whether cash flows survive into 2027.</strong>

<strong>Red Team:</strong> If the May–June exit-rate collapses and daily sales converge to \~10K, the "7.5M is the floor" thesis weakens significantly. <strong>The slope of deceleration in May–June matters more than the absolute sales total.</strong>

### Thesis 2: Free Updates Are Not About Free Content — They're About Decay-Rate Defense

<strong>Classification: Quality Compounder validation stage</strong>

The April–June update roadmap isn't a simple QoL bundle. Boss re-challenges, reconquest systems, and difficulty settings <strong>restart the endgame loop</strong>. New skills, pets, mounts, dedicated storage, and UI/control improvements <strong>target session length and return rates</strong>. Pearl Abyss explicitly stated these updates reflect player feedback and will roll out sequentially from April through June.

The effects are already observable. Over a 48-hour tracking window following recent updates, we recorded: <strong>peak concurrent users up 35.9% (146K → 198K)</strong>, <strong>trough concurrent up 72.5% (75.5K → 130.2K)</strong>, <strong>US sales rank improving from #5 to #2</strong>, <strong>China from #8 to #4</strong>, and <strong>new review positivity at 94%</strong>. This is evidence — not proof, but evidence — that update cadence and feedback integration are meaningfully influencing short-term sell-through and retention.

From an investor's perspective, the value of free updates is <strong>not direct revenue but incremental base-game sales</strong>. With sensitivity at \~KRW 10,000 per million copies, if the update program pushes an additional 300K–600K cumulative sales in 2026, that's <strong>KRW 3,000–6,000 in share-price support</strong>. That's the first-order effect.

The second-order effect is larger. When the market starts believing "this isn't a one-and-done package — they're building a live franchise through 2027," <strong>the multiple itself changes</strong>. Our model leaves room for <strong>PER +2–3x upon formal DLC/multiplayer roadmap announcement</strong>.

Paid DLC direct cash flows aren't negligible either. Under speculative assumptions — installed base of 9–12M, DLC attach rate of 15–30%, net unit price of KRW 20,000–30,000 — direct DLC revenue ranges from <strong>KRW 27 billion to KRW 108 billion</strong>. But we view the more important signal as <strong>franchise validation</strong> rather than the DLC revenue itself. Recent Reddit-based sentiment research also shows net-positive sentiment with strong demand for DLC/expansion content.

<strong>Red Team:</strong> The company itself noted these features are still in development and subject to change. Schedule delays, below-expectation quality, or weak attach rates would break the "free updates = franchise building" narrative.

### Thesis 3: The Engine Is Proven — Dokabi Is Still an Option

<strong>Classification: Idiosyncratic Alpha</strong>

BlackSpace Engine has graduated beyond "pretty demo engine." In its launch press release, Pearl Abyss highlighted the engine's photorealistic graphics and environmental interactivity. At GDC 2025, the company presented <strong>seamless open-world loading, detailed physics/environment interaction, and dynamic combat</strong> as core technical achievements. The company invested <strong>KRW 61.2 billion in R&D (37.5% of revenue)</strong> in H1 2025 and <strong>KRW 132.9 billion</strong> in the prior year on engine development — specifically on ray tracing, rendering quality, physics implementation, and large-scale open-world optimization. This is no longer a tech demo. It's <strong>commercial AAA production infrastructure</strong>.

But a common mistake follows: <strong>equating "proven engine" with "Unreal-like platform business" is overvaluation.</strong> The engine's direct value is modest. A more conservative IP SOTP puts <strong>Dokabi at KRW 0.2 trillion and engine/other options at KRW 0.1 trillion</strong> as a base case. Combined, that's still <strong>far smaller than the base game's long-tail value</strong>. The upside is still Crimson Desert.

Dokabi expectations follow the same logic. At the March 27 AGM, management said they're "preparing to showcase Dokabi quickly after Crimson Desert" and will "reveal development progress at the appropriate time." However, a separate February conference call report estimated <strong>roughly two years from Crimson Desert's launch to Dokabi's release</strong>. The conclusion: <strong>a reveal may be close, but actual earnings contribution is distant.</strong>

The implication for valuation is clear: <strong>Dokabi is not the main thesis that replaces the base game — it's option value worth several hundred billion won at most.</strong> A playable demo and rough timeline could compress the discount rate in the short term. But <strong>pricing in more than KRW 1 trillion of Dokabi value before proof is sentiment trading, not analysis</strong>. If the base game's long-tail weakens, Dokabi cannot compensate. This is the market's key mispricing variable.
