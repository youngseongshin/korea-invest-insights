## 1. Break the Conventional Wisdom: SpaceX Is Not a "Space Stock"

Viewing the SpaceX IPO solely as a "space-themed listing" misses the point. Where [the earlier piece](/post/spacex-ipo-korea-market-liquidity-ai-space-readthrough-2026-06-05/) examined the liquidity angle, this one looks at <strong>where the money is actually spent</strong>. The answer is in the S-1 itself.

SpaceX filed a Form S-1 with the SEC on May 20, 2026, and an amendment on June 1. Selling roughly 555.6m shares at $135 per share, it aims to <strong>raise about $75bn</strong> at a target valuation of roughly $1.75tn — the largest IPO in U.S. history. ([CNBC][1])

The crux is not the size but the <strong>use</strong>. According to multiple analyses, the S-1's use-of-proceeds plan places <strong>"AI compute infrastructure expansion" ahead of</strong> the launch business, with most of the capital allocated to AI compute infrastructure, launch capacity, and Starlink build-out. ([KraneShares][2])

---

## 2. Dissecting the Use of Proceeds

### 2.1 The Biggest Capex and Loss Center Is xAI

SpaceX's ownership structure makes the picture clear. <strong>SpaceX owns xAI, and xAI owns X (formerly Twitter)</strong>, with the AI data center and chip-related businesses sitting beneath. And the largest operating loss and capex sit precisely in this AI segment. xAI <strong>lost roughly $6bn in 2025 and burned about $2.5bn in Q1 2026 alone</strong>. ([Via Satellite][3])

Capex itself has gone vertical.

```text
SpaceX total capex trajectory
2023: ~$4.4bn
2025: ~$20.7bn
Q1 2026: ~$10.1bn (single quarter)
```

This is not the curve of a "rocket company." It is the curve of a company building AI compute and a satellite network at the same time. ([Morningstar][4])

### 2.2 "AI Compute" Is Not an Abstraction — It Is Physical Data Centers

SpaceX's AI compute runs on two tracks.

1. <strong>Ground data centers</strong>: GPU clusters for xAI's training and inference. This is where enormous GPU, HBM, and power demand originates.
2. <strong>Orbital data centers (AI1)</strong>: SpaceX COO Gwynne Shotwell said the company plans to launch the first AI1 unit in late 2027, and before that will put compute onboard Starlink broadband and mobile satellites. ([DCD][5])

So the proceeds are "money going to space" and at the same time <strong>"money going to AI compute."</strong> The two are not separate — Starlink's launch capacity itself becomes the delivery vehicle for orbital compute infrastructure.

## 3. Why This Is "AI Infrastructure Acceleration"

This is where the hypothesis gets tested. The funding relay laid out in [the AI supercycle extension piece](/post/ai-supercycle-extension-agent-demand-ipo-funding-memory-storage-2026-06-12/) — Big Tech cash → corporate bonds → <strong>public markets</strong> — sees its third stage actually open with the SpaceX IPO.

The core logic goes like this.

* xAI is <strong>burning cash fast</strong> right now ($6bn loss in 2025). The fuel for a loss-making AI compute business is, ultimately, financing.
* While at the private stage that fuel came from inside Musk's ecosystem and private placements, <strong>the SpaceX IPO opens a new funding source: the public market.</strong>
* If part of the $75bn flows into AI compute, that translates directly into <strong>orders for GPUs, HBM, server memory, eSSD, and power equipment</strong>.

In other words, the more successful the SpaceX IPO, the more entrenched a structure in which AI compute capex is hard to stop. <strong>This is the mechanism of "successful IPO → AI infrastructure acceleration."</strong>

> Let's be honest about the distinction, though. The proceeds may create new capex, or they may merely backstop already-planned capex financially. The direction of "acceleration" is right, but its magnitude must be confirmed by actual execution after listing.

## 4. Today's Link: The Chey-Musk Meeting

The point where the hypothesis connects to Korea was reported today. Digital Daily reported exclusively that <strong>SK Chairman Chey Tae-won and Elon Musk are set to meet in the U.S. in late June</strong>. An SK Group official's position is that this is "hard to confirm." ([Digital Daily][6])

The reported agenda is precisely the Korean end of the mechanism above.

| Cooperation Area | Details | Korean Memory Link |
|---|---|---|
| Semiconductors | Exploratory talks on custom HBM for Tesla's in-house AI5/AI6 chips, HBM4 base die cooperation | SK hynix / Samsung Electronics HBM |
| AI Infrastructure | Memory chips needed for xAI data center expansion | Server DRAM / HBM / eSSD |
| Space & Comms | Memory and storage for SpaceX spacecraft and low-orbit satellites | High-reliability memory / storage |
| Energy | ESS (energy storage systems) for data centers and the power grid | Power / battery value chain |

One official assessed that "SK Group is broadening the scope of cooperation to seize leadership in next-generation AI infrastructure." ([Digital Daily][6])

The crux is this. The <strong>"public-market capital flowing to AI compute"</strong> created by the SpaceX IPO meets the <strong>"HBM and memory that AI compute requires"</strong> that SK holds — at the same table. The starting point of the capital (the IPO) and its destination (Korean memory) enter the same picture.

## 5. Korea Read-Through (Illustrative Watch Points)

What follows is not a stock recommendation but an <strong>illustration</strong> of where to look first when the mechanism above actually starts working.

* <strong>SK hynix</strong> — the most direct name in this link. The HBM leader and a core supply candidate for the meeting's agenda (custom HBM for Tesla, memory for xAI data centers). That said, custom HBM is still at the "exploratory" stage, not an order.
* <strong>Samsung Electronics</strong> — a composite option of HBM4 base die, custom-chip foundry, and data center eSSD. If the chips in Musk's ecosystem (AI5/AI6) use an external foundry, the option value rises.
* <strong>Power / ESS / cooling</strong> — AI compute is, in the end, a power problem. ESS being on the meeting agenda overlaps exactly with the "the bottleneck after GPUs is power" from [the data center CapEx piece](/post/ai-datacenter-capex-5p3t-korea-power-substrate-storage-bottleneck-2026-06-05/).

> Common condition: for these names to become candidates, "a hot SpaceX IPO" alone is not enough. <strong>Actual use-of-proceeds execution, official confirmation of the meeting, and conversion of custom HBM into orders</strong> must all arrive together.

## 6. Red Team: When This Hypothesis Weakens

* <strong>Capital reallocation vs new capex</strong>: if the proceeds merely backfill already-planned investment financially, the incremental "acceleration" is small.
* <strong>xAI loss risk</strong>: AI compute is a massive cash-burning sink ($6bn loss in 2025). Even if listing proceeds cover it, late monetization raises the burden of follow-on financing.
* <strong>Unconfirmed meeting</strong>: the Chey-Musk meeting is a sole-source report, and SK's position is "hard to confirm." The custom HBM on the agenda is also exploratory. It is too early to price it as fact.
* <strong>Orbital data centers are long-dated</strong>: AI1 is slated to launch in late 2027. The Korean memory upside from "satellite compute" is an option, not near-term earnings.
* <strong>IPO funding-drain backlash</strong>: as seen in [the earlier piece](/post/spacex-ipo-korea-market-liquidity-ai-space-readthrough-2026-06-05/), if a large IPO becomes a funding source by selling existing AI positions, Samsung Electronics and SK hynix could become sell targets in the short term. The material for acceleration is also the material for short-term volatility.
